Introduction

Purpose of this document

Electrical Engineering is an expansive field. Finding your passion and figuring out how best to pursue it can be challenging as a new Course 6-1 undergraduate. This guide aims to provide a roadmap for navigating EE courses, research, and internships. It is based on input, data, and advice from former and current 6-1s. We hope this guide will help to shed some light on your path as an EE at MIT.

What is EE?

Consider a laptop, which must possess an array of functions including storage, connectivity, display. At some level, Electrical Engineering is involved in the development of each of these functions. Engineers tasked with designing the memory would need to understand the physics of nanoscale MOS devices and be able to design circuits for the latest CMOS process. RF and wireless engineers would perform EM simulations of antennas, design the radio frequency chips, and decide which wireless protocols to support in the baseband SoC. Finally, individuals working on the display technology might solve problems that draw on optics and organic chemistry expertise.

Key takeaways from this are that EE

  • is very cross-disciplinary
  • deals with challenging problems at the fundamental level (ex. new materials, processes), component level (ex. semiconductors), and system level (ex. radios)
  • provides the technology for critical capabilities.

A quick search for “EE jobs” yields the following job results:

  • Display/Flexible Electronics - Research Analyst (Lux Research Inc.)
  • Signal Processing Engineer (MITRE)
  • Entry-Level IC Design Engineer (Maxim Integrated)
  • Senior Applications Engineer (Analog Devices)
  • RF / Microwave System Engineer (Starry Inc.)

There is no shortage of demand for EEs. Though a particular technology or trend may go out of style, the mindset that EE provides is amenable to a variety of new challenges.

Where is EE going?

The following is a sample of current industry and research trends. We hope that some of the exciting problems described below inspire you to learn more.

  • Nanofabrication of silicon ICs is facing daunting obstacles to further scaling beyond the 14nm technology node. So called “more-than-Moore” technologies like 3D transistors (FinFET or tri-gate) and new materials (III-V semiconductors and graphene) are being actively developed. This problem is important because the economics of Moore’s Law will break down if scaling stops and improvements in computation slow.
  • 5G, the next generation of wireless, is expected to bring about 10x improvements in data rates (10 GBPS) and capacity as it is deployed over the next five years. Researchers are currently simulating 5G networks, developing specifications for the air interface, and lobbying the FCC. The shift to millimeter-wave frequencies (>28GHz) will require innovative high-speed RF circuits and smart signal processing for MIMO.
  • Lab-on-chip devices could provide integrated solutions for chemical sensing and detection of biological markers. IBM, for instance, recently demonstrated a chip based on microfluidics that could detect early stage cancer cells. Terahertz spectrometers in standard CMOS show potential for sensing explosives.

How to EE at MIT?

In 2015, there were 25 enrolled EE sophomores making 6-1 the least popular Course 6 program with only a 6.25% share. It’s no surprise then that, without a large community of peers, navigating EE at MIT can sometimes be a challenge. This is why Voltage was created. Here are some tips, tricks, and recommendations from members of the 6-1 community for making the most of the experience:

  • Do a UROP(s)
  • Attend Voltage events and talk to fellow 6-1s! Subscribe to ee-chat@mit.edu
  • Take the foundation classes ASAP, but also try to get a header in early on
  • When interviewing, demonstrate interest in technical and industry trends
  • Work on projects or engineering teams outside class to gain practical experience
  • Read trade journals to gain an appreciation for the current problems in EE
  • Don’t limit yourself to EE; explore relevant course offerings in 8, 10, other 6, etc.

Disciplines

Circuits

Circuits harness electrical phenomena to process signals and power. They are the backbone of complex digital systems, wireless transceivers, and power converters. Circuit design requires a holistic approach that integrates system architecture, passive components/EM techniques, and device technology. As the underlying technologies advance, circuits are improved to provide better performance in terms of power consumption, noise/linearity,  speed, cost, etc.

BioEECS

Bioscience (including both fundamental biology and medicine) is arguably the most important engineering system that mankind wishes to model, understand, and engineer.  EECS provides a rich set of analytical tools to tackle these systems.  EECS is applied to both bio-hardware (e.g., cells, tissues, organisms) and bio-software (e.g., proteomes) and EECS uses both "traditional" hardware (e.g., micro/nanotechnology) and software (e.g., computational biophysics) to model, understand, and engineer biological systems.

(paraphrased from http://eecsoldweb.mit.edu/bioeecs/)

Applied Physics and Devices

What is Applied Physics and Devices about? Is ApplPhysDev about engineering or is it about physics? The simple answer is that ApplPhysDev has both, and a multitude of both in numerous multidisciplinary fields, including chemistry, materials science, mathematics, physics, electrical engineering, biology and bioelectrical engineering, and in mechanical engineering too! ApplPhysDev uses the foundation and underlying principles of physics to enable the engineering of complex integrated systems. For a few examples, the systems under investigation span electronics, optoelectronics, photonics, lasers, plasma fusion, quantum communication and computation, microelectromechanical and microfluidic structures.

-Excerpt from http://www.eecs.mit.edu/grad-areas/1-applphysdev/

Information Systems

Lying at the critical interface between computation and the physical world, Information Systems bridges the more traditionally computer science centric and more traditionally electrical engineering centric areas of the department. The research ranges from fundamental system, information, and network science, through to engineering principles and design for a host of important contemporary and emerging applications. Examples include a broad spectrum of problems of communication and coding, systems theory and control, optimization, statistical inference and decision theory, and signal processing, as well as the shared methodological underpinnings of—and increasingly the interactions between—these different fields. Research topics range from fundamental principles to application, from analysis to synthesis, and from theory to experiment and simulation. Thesis research can involve different combinations of the above, depending on the student's interests and the nature of the problem.

-Excerpt from Infosys <http://www.eecs.mit.edu/grad-areas/1-infosys/index.html>

Digital and Embedded Systems

Embedded systems/microcontrollers are designed for specific tasks, sometimes with real-time performance constraints in mind. They provide flexibility, real-time control and robustness and are found in a variety of items, such as thermostats, calculators, robots, etc. Digital circuits (think Apple’s latest application processor) perform logic operations and are optimized for the latest silicon (or otherwise) manufacturing technology.
